The Fundamentals.

Registration, permits, camp fees, fuel maths, communications, and the community. The handbook that makes the previous three articles legal.

Reference Current to autumn 2026 Verify before riding

Half the questions on the Adventure Riders Brisbane Facebook group are the same six: do I need a permit, can I take my unregistered bike, how much is camping, where do I get fuel, will my phone work, and where do I find other people to ride with. This is the answer key.

Rules in this country are nationally consistent in spirit but locally specific in detail. The Queensland Parks & Wildlife Service, HQPlantations, NSW NPWS and NSW Forestry Corporation each have their own rulebook, fee schedule, and enforcement appetite. This article distils the relevant details for the destinations in this atlas. If you're riding somewhere not covered here, verify with the relevant authority before you go.

§ 01Registration & licensing

The single most important rule, repeated again because it is the one most often broken: every motorcycle ridden in a Queensland or NSW national park, state forest or HQP plantation must be fully road-registered. Conditional, recreational, or "rec" rego is not legal on these roads. Your motorcycle licence must be current. Normal road rules apply to every gazetted forest road.

Per QPWS: "Trail bikes must be registered for general road use. Riders must hold a current driving licence." HQPlantations is more pointed: their website explicitly notes that "if you're caught riding without [registration], you will face heavy penalties including the loss of your motorbike."

Why this is enforced QPWS rangers do patrol. NSW NPWS rangers patrol harder. The fines are not academic — typically several hundred dollars per offence plus the cost of vehicle impoundment. Insurance is also voided. The cost-saving of conditional rego is dwarfed by one infringement.

§ 02Camping & park fees

Both QPWS and NSW NPWS charge for camping. Most NSW national parks also charge a per-vehicle day-use entry fee. The fees are modest — the inconvenience is in the booking, which must be done online in advance for almost every campground in this atlas.

QPWS camping (QLD)
$7.50 per person per night, or $30 per family per night (effective 1 July). Children under 5 free. Book online via book.parks.qld.gov.au — most sites cannot be paid for at the campground. Self-registration envelopes exist at some remote sites but bookings are still required.
QPWS vehicle entry
No daily vehicle fee for most parks in this atlas. (Some commercial / day-use parks differ.)
NSW NPWS vehicle entry
$8 per vehicle per day in fee-paying parks (Border Ranges, parts of Gibraltar Range). Exact cash, envelope system at park entrances. A Multi Park Pass ($65–$190/year depending on type) is better value if you'll be in NSW parks more than 4–5 times a year. Book and pay via nationalparks.nsw.gov.au.
NSW NPWS camping
Variable by site. Typically $12–$25 per adult per night. Book online.
HQPlantations
No fee for individual riders. Organised motorised groups of 5+ riders require an "organised event permit", free, apply via hqplantations.com.au, lead time of several weeks.
NSW State Forests
Free to ride and camp at most state forest sites. Fire restrictions and seasonal closures apply. Check NSW Forestry Corporation site.

§ 03Fuel planning

Fuel range is the single most under-estimated variable for new adventure riders. A DR-Z 400 on a stock 7.6 L tank has a usable range of about 160 km — which is exactly the distance from Glen Innes to Grafton on the Old Glen Innes Road, with no margin and no fuel. A DR650 stretches that to 230 km. A T700 to 350 km. A GSA to 500 km. Plan to your bike, not the average.

The fuel gaps that catch people out
  • Glen Innes → Grafton (Old Glen Innes Rd) — ~160 km, no fuel. Carry spare on a sub-300 km-range bike.
  • Jimna → Kenilworth — ~70 km, no fuel anywhere in Jimna. Top up at Kilcoy on the way in.
  • Bunya Mountains — no fuel on the mountain itself. Refuel at Kingaroy or Maidenwell before climbing.
  • Border Ranges — no fuel inside the park. Kyogle (south) or Murwillumbah (north).
  • Mt Mee village — assume there is no fuel here. Refuel at Dayboro or Woodford.

The Australian convention is to refuel at every opportunity in remote country. Take it seriously. If you're on a small-tank bike, a 4 L Rotopax mounted to the rear rack costs ~$120 and solves the problem for almost every destination in this atlas. A folded fuel bladder (Giant Loop, MSR) is the lighter, cheaper alternative.

§ 04Communications & safety

Mobile coverage in this country is best-described as patchy by carrier. Telstra is dominant; Optus is acceptable on the highways and fails almost everywhere in the forest; the Vodafone / TPG network is functionally absent in most of this atlas's territory. If you're not already on Telstra, consider a prepaid Telstra SIM for the weekend.

Telstra
Works at parts of Jimna, most of Mt Mee, all of Beerburrum, all of the bucket-list route's towns. Patchy in Conondale, Gheerulla, the Tweed Range, and most of the Old Glen Innes Road.
Optus
Reliable in towns. Almost entirely absent in the forests.
PLB / EPIRB
A 406 MHz Personal Locator Beacon is the cheapest insurance you can buy. ResQLink+ floats; ACR ResQLink is the standard. ~$300, ten-year battery, free registration with AMSA. Carry one if riding solo.
Satellite messenger
Garmin inReach Mini 2 or SPOT Gen4. ~$400 + subscription. Allows two-way text. Worth it for the bucket-list trip and any solo overnight ride.
UHF CB
UHF Channel 40 is the truckie channel. Channel 5/35 is the emergency channel (do not use casually). Channel 10 is the 4WD channel. Channel 18 is RV/caravan. A handheld UHF is useful for group rides and warning of oncoming traffic on blind gravel.
First aid
Compact wilderness first aid kit, snake bandage (mandatory in this country — eastern brown is the local star), trauma shears. Keep it accessible — not in the bottom of the panniers.

§ 05Seasons & weather

Southeast Queensland and northern NSW are sub-tropical. The seasons that matter are wet (Dec–Feb) and dry (Apr–Sep). Autumn (March–May) is the textbook adventure-riding window: mid-20s daytime, cool nights, the soil dried out from summer, the bush still green. Spring (Aug–Oct) is the second-best window but carries an elevated fire-risk profile.

Wet season (Dec — Feb)
Avoid Conondale and Mt Mee. Causeways flood. Red clay impassable. Storms severe and fast. Forecast obsessively; check QPWS alerts page.
Autumn (Mar — May)
Optimal. Plan most of this atlas's trips for this window.
Winter (Jun — Aug)
Cold mornings, dry days, brilliant light. Excellent for the bucket-list loop. Bring an extra layer for Tenterfield mornings — can hit 0°C.
Spring (Sep — Nov)
Good riding. Watch for total fire ban days — no campfires anywhere, and you can be turned around at park entrances on extreme-rating days.
Forecasting
Bureau of Meteorology (bom.gov.au) is the only weather authority worth using. Willyweather is fine for nice-to-know detail.

§ 06Bike & gear notes

Every destination in this atlas is rideable on anything from a 250 cc dual-sport to a 1300 cc adventure tourer. Bike choice mostly affects fatigue, tyre life, and how much rain you can tolerate before things become unpleasant. A few practical notes:

Tyres

For 80% of the destinations here, a 50/50 tyre like the Heidenau K60 Scout, Mitas E07+, or Motoz Tractionator GPS is the right answer. For Gheerulla and the wetter Conondale tracks, lean more aggressive (60/40 or 70/30). For the bucket-list trip on a big bike, K60s are the consensus pick.

Standing on the pegs

The single biggest skill upgrade for new ADV riders. Lower your centre of gravity, weight the outside peg through corners, let the bike move under you. Find a parking lot. Practise until it feels normal. Then take it to gravel.

Luggage

Soft luggage forgives more than hard. For weekend trips, a 40 L dry-bag strapped to the rear rack covers most cases. For the bucket-list loop, a pair of Mosko Moto Backcountry 25 L panniers + tank bag is the popular Brisbane setup.

The basics

Tyre repair kit (plugs, CO2). Tube-type bikes: spare tube, irons, pump. Tools to fit your bike. Cable ties. Duct tape. A folded $50 in your wallet — country servos are not always card-friendly.

§ 07The community

Nobody good rides this country alone for long. The Brisbane ADV scene is active, generous, and organised — it just lives mostly on Facebook and in a few private forums, which can make it invisible from outside. Here's where to find it.

Adventure Riders Brisbane
Facebook group. Several thousand members. Weekly Sunday rides advertised here, plus the constant stream of "where did you ride this weekend" photo posts that will give you ideas for the next six months.
DSMRA
The Dual Sport Motorcycle Riders' Association. National. $50/year membership. Organised weekend rides, training days, social events. dsmra.org.au.
RideADV
Commercial guided tour operator based in NSW. Multi-day trips through this country, all bikes welcome, support vehicle. Good for one-off bucket-list trips. rideadv.com.au.
RME Recmoto
Sydney-based dual-sport club running occasional Border Ranges day tours from the NSW side. recmoto.com.au.
Two Wheels Motorcycle Tours
Brisbane-based guided tour operator. Mostly road tours, some adventure offerings.
Cape York Motorcycle Adventures
For when you've outgrown this atlas. Cairns-based; runs the Cape York Telegraph Track and Old Coach Road trips that are the next-step bucket-list rides.
advrider.com — Australia subforum
Long-running global ADV forum. The Australia subforum has detailed ride reports going back twenty years for almost every road in this atlas. Searchable archive.
Dualsport Australia
Curated GPX route packs and ride guides for sale across the continent. Worth the small fee for the bucket-list loop alone.
The annual events
Suzuki DR & Adventure Bike Gathering (Jimna, autumn), Variety Adventure Ride (~1,780 km, six-day charity ride, varies), Distinguished Gentleman's Ride (May, Brisbane, sealed but a community-building day).

§ 08Maps & navigation

Phone GPS is fine for 90% of the rides in this atlas. For the bucket-list loop and any solo overnight ride, supplement with a dedicated GPS or a paper map.

Paper
HEMA New England High Country NSW Motorcycle Touring map — produced with BMW Motorrad Australia. The single best paper reference for the NSW side of any Brisbane ADV trip. Buy before you go. Hema Maps also do Great Desert Tracks (overkill for SEQ, essential for outback).
Digital — phone
Gaia GPS (subscription, excellent offline maps), Maps.me (free, offline), or Google Maps in offline mode. Cache the route before you leave service.
Digital — dedicated GPS
Garmin Zumo XT2 or BMW Navigator VI mount cleanly on most ADV bikes. Worth it for repeat trips.
GPX packs
Dualsport Australia sells curated GPX route packs for many of the destinations in this atlas. Worth the modest fee.

§ 09The Bicentennial National Trail — a clarification

The Bicentennial National Trail (BNT) is a 5,330 km horse-and-walker trail from Cooktown to Healesville, passing through much of the country covered in this atlas. It is not legal to ride a motorbike on it. Per the National Trail's own published rules: "The Trail is not open to motorised vehicles or trail bikes, and pets are not permitted."

Some forum threads on advrider.com discuss whether sections might be rideable. The answer is no. What ADV riders actually do is ride the parallel gravel back-roads that shadow the BNT corridor through Blackbutt, Ravensbourne, Crows Nest, Killarney and beyond. Many of those roads appear in Articles II and III of this atlas. Treat the BNT as a reference line, not a route.

A FINAL NOTE

Everything in this atlas was current at the time of going to press (autumn 2026). Park rules, camping fees, road closures and access permissions do change — usually annually, sometimes mid-season. Before any trip in this atlas, spend ten minutes on the official sources:

  • parks.qld.gov.au — QPWS rules, alerts, closures
  • book.parks.qld.gov.au — QPWS camping bookings
  • hqplantations.com.au — HQP plantation access
  • nationalparks.nsw.gov.au — NSW NPWS rules, fees, bookings
  • bom.gov.au — weather

The community on Adventure Riders Brisbane will answer almost any question within an hour if you post politely. Use them. Add to them. Ride safe.
