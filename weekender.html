The Weekender.

Friday afternoon out, Sunday afternoon home. Six destinations between two and four hours from Brisbane — the heart of the SEQ adventure-riding map.

2 – 4 hr drive 200 – 400 km loops Intermediate Mar — Oct preferred

The weekender is where Brisbane adventure riding lives. A Friday-night ride to a country pub, Saturday on the forestry roads, Sunday morning packing up and rolling home through Maleny. It is the format the region was built for.

What follows are six destinations that sit in the sweet spot between two and four hours from the CBD. Each one will carry a full Saturday of riding without retracing a single road. Each one has a campground or a pub you can base out of. And each one has been ridden hard enough by enough people that you'll find ride reports, GPX tracks, and Saturday-morning meet-ups on Facebook within a minute of looking.

We've ordered them roughly by distance, starting with the iconic SEQ weekender — Jimna — and moving outward. The pinnacle entry, the 26 km trail-bike-only Gheerulla track in Mapleton, is the only purpose-built dedicated trail-bike route in southeast Queensland. Make a pilgrimage.

A note on bike size Every destination in this article is rideable on anything from a DR-Z 400 to a BMW GS1250 Adventure. Bike size mainly affects fatigue, tyre choice, and how much rain you can tolerate before the red clay starts to win. The forest roads are big-bike friendly; the singletrack offshoots are not.
Destination No. 04

Jimna State Forest

The iconic SEQ weekender
Co-ordinates S 26° 40′   E 152° 27′
Distance from CBD 145 km · 90 min
PLATE 04 / JIMNA
S 26.67° / E 152.46° Jimna State Forest — Yabba Creek catchment

Jimna sits on the western edge of the Conondale Range, in hoop pine plantation and native forest. The 47-metre fire tower is Queensland's tallest. The Kilcoy → Jimna → Kenilworth loop is the iconic SEQ ADV weekender.

Surface Forest roads, some red clay
Difficulty Intermediate
Bike suitability DR650, T700, mid-size GS
Camp fee $7.50 pppn

If there is a spiritual home of SEQ adventure riding, it's Jimna. The forest road network runs south to Conondale via Bellthorpe and east to Kenilworth/Imbil, knitting together what is effectively a 200-km Saturday loop with no single road repeated. The forestry tracks vary from graded gravel suitable for a fully-loaded GSA to steep red-clay climbs that will defeat anything bigger than a DR650 after rain.

The two camping options are Peach Trees, the QPWS site beside Yabba Creek (grass, fire rings, treated-water tap, wheelchair-accessible toilet), and Jimna Base Camp, a private site at the old Jimna State School run by Pete and Marianne. Base Camp has hot showers, flush toilets, an on-site weekend kiosk doing barista coffee, fire pits, and a 11 pm noise curfew. It is the de-facto meeting point for the annual Suzuki DR & Adventure Bike Gathering.

No formal ride routes, no lead riders, no sweepers — participants ride self-guided from a maps pack.

That's the format of the Suzuki DR Gathering, now in its fifth year, hosted by Pete & Marianne in partnership with Northside Motorcycle Tyres & Service. Friday dinner at the Jimna Sports Club. Saturday breakfast under the old school rec building. Sunday morning, you pack up and ride home. If you're new to Brisbane ADV, this is the weekend to put in the diary.

The signature loop

Kilcoy → Mt Kilcoy Road → Jimna forest roads → Jimna fire tower (the tallest in QLD, 47 m total height) → Monsildale Road descent → Kenilworth (lunch at the Kenilworth Hotel or the Kenilworth Country Bakery, famed for its custard tart) → Maleny → Kilcoy. About 200 km, half of it unsealed. A full and rewarding Saturday.

Logistics

Fuel
Fill up at Kilcoy. No fuel at Jimna or Conondale. Next east is Kenilworth (~70 km from Jimna); next north is Yarraman/Kingaroy.
Camping — Peach Trees
QPWS. $7.50 pppn. Book via book.parks.qld.gov.au. Tap water (treat before drinking), wheelchair toilet, fire rings.
Camping — Jimna Base Camp
Private. ~$20/site. Hot showers, flush toilets, on-site weekend kiosk, fire pits. 11 pm noise curfew on weekends. Pet and horse friendly. Pete & Marianne, 0417 756 876.
Signature event
Annual Suzuki DR & Adventure Bike Gathering, hosted at Jimna Base Camp. Open to all makes. Self-guided.
Phone
Telstra works in parts. Optus patchy. Take a PLB if riding solo.
When to avoid
After heavy rain — the red-clay climbs become impassable. Forestry-truck weekdays during plantation operations.
Destination No. 05

Conondale & Imbil

National park, state forest, creek crossings
Co-ordinates S 26° 44′   E 152° 39′
Distance from CBD 175 km · 2 hr
PLATE 05 / BOOLOUMBA
S 26.73° / E 152.65° Booloumba Creek crossing

There are three creek crossings on the road into Booloumba Creek camp. Signage indicates 4WDs only. Trail bikes go through happily in low water — and absolutely not when the creek is up.

Surface Red clay, creek crossings, forest roads
Difficulty Intermediate
Bike suitability DR-Z, T700, KTM 500
Camp fee $7.50 pppn

Conondale National Park and Imbil State Forest share a fenceline with Jimna and complete the northern triangle of SEQ adventure country. The riding here is more technical: red-clay climbs on Grigors Road, the three Booloumba Creek crossings, and the climbing hairpins of Sunday Creek Road. After rain, this country becomes a different beast — even good dual-sport tyres struggle on wet Conondale clay.

The two campgrounds are Charlie Moreland on Sunday Creek Road and Booloumba Creek, both QPWS. Booloumba is the more popular weekend base because the loop home via Mt Mee or Maleny adds a satisfying Sunday-morning leg. The Mt Allan fire tower above Booloumba is closed to climbing through 27 May 2026, but the road up to it remains open and the views are excellent.

Imbil firing range The northern part of Imbil State Forest, west of Imbil township, contains an active firing range. Per QPWS: "know where the firing range is located and do not enter unless authorised." Heed the signage. The Charlie Moreland end of the forest is unaffected.

Logistics

Access
Via Kenilworth (Bruce Hwy → Eumundi → Kenilworth) or via Maleny / Conondale township. Either way the last 15 km is gravel.
Fuel
Kenilworth (east) or Maleny (south). No fuel inside the park.
Camping
Charlie Moreland (Sunday Creek Rd) or Booloumba Creek. QPWS, $7.50 pppn.
Pub stop
The Kenilworth Hotel. Big steaks, big parking lot, generous attitude toward dusty riders.
When to avoid
After > 50 mm rain in 24 hr, wait 48 hours before riding. The red Conondale clay is famously slippery.
Destination No. 06

Gheerulla

Mapleton National Park · The flagship
Co-ordinates S 26° 35′   E 152° 49′
Distance from CBD 120 km · 2 hr
PLATE 06 / GHEERULLA
S 26.59° / E 152.82° Gheerulla trail-bike track

Gheerulla is the only dedicated trail-bike-only track in southeast Queensland. No walkers. No bicycles. No 4WDs. Twenty-six kilometres of registered-bikes-only forest trail.

Surface Dedicated trail-bike track · 26 km
Difficulty Intermediate
Bike suitability DR-Z, KTM 500, DR650, T700
Permit / fee No special permit · Camp $7.50

Gheerulla is the unicorn: 26 km of forest trail dedicated solely to licensed riders on registered motorcycles. No special permit required. No walkers, no bicycles, no 4WDs. Just trail-bike riders. QPWS describes it as "about 26 km of forest trail riding experience" — which dramatically undersells how rare this is in Australia.

Access is via two points, both 2WD-accessible in dry conditions. The main western entry is from the Eumundi–Kenilworth Road, 24 km from the Bruce Highway, then 2 km in via Sam Kelly Road — which has a dedicated trail-bike parking and unloading area. The eastern entry is via Delicia Road and Mapleton Forest Road. Bring a printed copy of the QPWS Gheerulla trail bike track map (PDF, March 2025); the mix of one-way and two-way sections matters.

If you only do one purpose-built ride near Brisbane — make it Gheerulla.

The camping is the catch. Gheerulla camping area is a small walk-in site beside Gheerulla Creek with three sites, a toilet, and not much else. The walk-in is 900 m from the Sam Kelly carpark. Mobile coverage poor (a QPWS warning). For most weekend riders the move is to base out of Kenilworth and ride to Gheerulla as a Saturday round-trip.

Logistics

Access
West: Eumundi–Kenilworth Rd → Sam Kelly Rd (trail-bike parking). East: Delicia Rd → Mapleton Forest Rd.
Fuel
Kenilworth (west) or Mapleton (east).
Camping
Gheerulla camping area, walk-in 900 m from Sam Kelly carpark. Three sites only. Toilet, no other facilities. Book online.
Phone
Poor at the camping area. Telstra patchy. Plan accordingly.
When to avoid
QPWS: "During severe wet weather, which occurs mostly in spring and summer, some tracks flood and are closed for public safety." Check alerts before riding.
Destination No. 07

Yarraman & Benarkin

Brisbane Valley state forest network
Co-ordinates S 26° 51′   E 151° 59′
Distance from CBD 180 km · 2.5 hr
PLATE 07 / YARRAMAN
S 26.86° / E 151.98° Yarraman State Forest

The Brisbane Valley network — Yarraman, Benarkin, Blackbutt — links to the longer Bunya–Cooyar corridor. The Blackbutt Bakery is the social hub.

Surface Plantation forest roads, sealed connectors
Difficulty Beginner → Intermediate
Bike suitability Any ADV bike
Permit / fee None for individuals

QPWS recommends the roads "zigzagging through Benarkin, Yarraman and Lockyer" as "ideal for a day ride." They aren't kidding. The Brisbane Valley network is the most underrated zone in this atlas — easy gravel, light traffic, no clay, and on the Maidenwell side it links to the Bunya Mountains for a glorious multi-day loop. Blackbutt Bakery is the social hub, legendary among both cyclists and ADV riders.

The full weekend itinerary is Brisbane → D'Aguilar Hwy → Yarraman → Blackbutt → Maidenwell → Cooyar (stop at Garry McCoy's servo — former GP rider, premium fuel, the man wears a helmet rule does not apply here) → Bunya Mountains for the night → home via Crows Nest. A 600-km weekend with 50 per cent unsealed.

A note on the Brisbane Valley Rail Trail The 161 km Yarraman–Wulkuraka rail trail is the longest in Queensland and Australia — but it is closed to motorbikes. Bicycles only. The parallel gravel back-roads through Pidna and South Nanango State Forests are excellent ADV territory and run beside the rail trail for much of its length.

Logistics

Fuel
Yarraman, Blackbutt, Cooyar (Garry McCoy's), Maidenwell. No long fuel gaps.
Stops
Blackbutt Bakery (mandatory). Yarraman Hotel. Linville Hotel. Maidenwell Hotel.
Accommodation
Yarraman Caravan Park has cabins. Most riders push on to the Bunya Mountains and stay in a cabin there.
Multi-day add-on
Yarraman → Maidenwell → Bunya Mountains → Kingaroy → Goomeri → Murgon → return. A 700 km, 3-day loop with serious gravel.
Destination No. 08

Bunya Mountains

via Kingaroy or Maidenwell
Co-ordinates S 26° 53′   E 151° 35′
Distance from CBD 230 km · 3 hr
PLATE 08 / BUNYA MTNS
S 26.89° / E 151.58° Bunya Mountains National Park

The Bunyas are mostly a road-riding destination once on the mountain itself, but the eastern approach via Maidenwell delivers 4 km of properly corrugated dirt — adventure-bikes-only when wet.

Surface Sealed mountain road · corrugated approach
Difficulty Beginner
Bike suitability Any bike
Camp fee $7.50 pppn

The Bunyas are not a hard adventure destination on the mountain itself — once you're up there, it's sealed 60 km/h roads and hundreds of wallabies. The appeal is the approach: the eastern Maidenwell road still has about 4 km of corrugated dirt that, per webBikeWorld, is "rideable, but will make your bike dirty and, if it rains, is restricted to adventure bikes only." That, plus the plantation roads linking the Bunyas south to Cooyar, makes for a worthy weekend base.

Once up the mountain, you have three QPWS campgrounds (Dandabah, Westcott, Burtons Well) and around a hundred private cabins. Dandabah convenience store, Poppies on the Hill café, Elz Bistro, Lyrics Restaurant, and Shackletons Bar (113 varieties of whiskey, per their own signage). The Russell Park MTB trails are non-motorised and irrelevant for ADV riders.

Logistics

Fuel
No fuel on the mountain. Refuel at Kingaroy, Kumbia, or Maidenwell.
Cabins
Bunya Mountains Accommodation Centre, (07) 4668 3126.
The approach worth doing
Maidenwell → Bunya Mountains Road (east side) → summit. The 4 km corrugated section is your toll.
Hazards
Wallabies. Dawn and dusk are particularly dense. Ride accordingly.
Destination No. 09

Lockyer & Crows Nest

Toowoomba range & Garry McCoy's servo
Co-ordinates S 27° 16′   E 152° 03′
Distance from CBD 150 km · 2 hr
PLATE 09 / LOCKYER
S 27.27° / E 152.05° Lockyer National Park

Lockyer National Park sits on the eastern face of the Toowoomba range. Crows Nest, north of Toowoomba, is a designated Motorcycle Friendly Town.

Surface Mixed gravel and sealed back-roads
Difficulty Beginner → Intermediate
Bike suitability Any ADV bike
Permit / fee None

Lockyer National Park is the QPWS-listed companion to Benarkin and Yarraman: "ideal for a day ride." The riding here is gentler than Conondale and the views from the range edge are dramatic. Crows Nest, north of Toowoomba, is officially designated a Motorcycle Friendly Town and is the natural overnight base. The classic day-ride is Brisbane → Mt Crosby → Fernvale → Esk → Crows Nest, with the return via Garry McCoy's Cooyar servo for fuel and a yarn.

Logistics

Fuel
Esk, Crows Nest, Cooyar, Toowoomba. No long gaps.
Accommodation
Crows Nest Pub, Crows Nest Caravan Park. Toowoomba for more options.
Famous stop
Garry McCoy's Cooyar servo. Former GP rider. Premium 98 octane. Stop and chat.
WEEKENDER LOOPS AT A GLANCE
The introduction
Jimna + Base Camp. Camp Friday, ride the Kilcoy → Jimna → Kenilworth loop Saturday, home Sunday. The benchmark.
The pilgrimage
Gheerulla. 26 km of trail-bike-only forest track. Stay in Kenilworth, ride to Sam Kelly Road, ride the loop both directions.
The technical one
Conondale Booloumba Creek. Three crossings. Red clay. Reward.
The long day
Brisbane Valley → Bunya Mountains. Yarraman, Blackbutt Bakery, Maidenwell, Bunyas. 600 km weekend.
The cool-climate one
Lockyer + Crows Nest. Toowoomba range, MX-friendly town, Garry McCoy's servo.
When to avoid
Wet season (Dec–Feb): Conondale clay impassable, creeks flood. Total fire ban days: no campfires anywhere. Plantation operations: weekday logging trucks at Jimna and Beerburrum.
